## What To Read

These are my two main inspirations for my approach. I've read other books, but
these are the two I keep coming back to as a reference.

- "Daniels Running Formula" by Jack Daniels
- "Faster Road Racing" by Pete Pfitzinger

## Run More, Run Slow Enough

Basically my approach is to maximise the amount I can run, in function of the
time you can / are willing to spend on it and in terms of what the body can
handle. This comes first and foremost in my advice for two reasons. One, I just like
running, so I want to maximise my time spent doing it. Two, more running builds
your endurance. You need endurance to make the rest work. People also claim
that lifetime distance matters and I figure I have some catching up to do on
people that have been running most of their life.

To be able to run more, you need to run easy enough. Shouldn't feel dead after
every run. If you just did an hour of easy running and I told you to continue
for another 30 minutes, it should be no problem. If the next morning, you don't
think you would be able to run an hour again (and the next morning, and the
next, and the next), then probably you run it too fast. Of course this depends
a bit on how much running you are used to, e.g., replace "hour" by "40 minutes"
in this paragraph.

Running 45 minutes on day one and another 45 minutes on day two is less intense
than running 1h30 on day one and taking a day off on day two. Longer runs do
have their own place and merit, but when simply defining your base, I would go
for the first option. It also enables you to maybe run 2×50 minutes instead.
Yay, more running.

My default easy run has sort of ended up being "about an hour". I don't recall
if there was any specific reason for this, but I do think it a nice cutoff
beyond which you maybe should start thinking about splitting up things into
another day.

## Heartrate

If you don't know how your body should feel when running easy, then heart rate
can be a proxy. There are different formulas for this, they don't all agree,
they don't all start from the same basis (some just use your max HR, some use
max HR + resting HR, some use lactate threshold HR). Don't use one based on
your age. It's shit, it's useless, it's made for population averages, it's not
for you.

First off, be sure your HR is read correctly. Chest strap is supposedly most
precise, optical readings are mostly OK these days. I use optical wrist. Some
older models had issues with cadence lock (so it would show your cadence
instead). It works with light, so too lose on the wrist means more outdoor
light can creep in, means probably less accurate readings. Check dcrainmaker
for a review on the watch you use, he tends to do a detailed HR reading
comparison. For runs, I put my watch a bit higher on the forearm (so the arm is
bigger than the wrist), and a bit tighter than day-to-day wearing. I know it
was advised for the previous model I used, I don't know if it is still the
case.

Next, find your max heart rate. (or if you want to use another formula, find
what they need). The advice I've usually seen for this is, do some warmup, go
find a hill, race up the hill as hard as you can. Jog back down, repeat. Do
this several times until it looks like your HR won't go up any more. Whatever
was the highest reading you got is your max HR. See something higher later on?
Bravo, that's a new max. It's no exact science. I've never actually done this
approach, at the time I raced often enough that I figured I had a good enough
idea of my max HR anyway.

Back when I paid more attention to HR, I used the formula in "Faster Road
Racing". That one also requires your resting HR. If your watch does constant
reading, then you can probably find that data in the watch or the accompanying
app. Can also try a quick reading when you wake up. None of it will be super
precise. Again, not exact science. If it's all in a reasonable ballpark, you
should get a good idea of HR though.

Throw the numbers in [a calculator](/calc/), whichever you want to use. For
example the faster road racing one says regular running should fall in the
range of `resting + (0.62 * hrr)` to `resting + (0.75 * hrr)`, where `hrr` is
your heart rate reserve, the difference between your maximum and your resting
heart rate. For a long run, more heart rate drift can occur and they define the
maximum for easy running at `resting + (0.78 * hrr)` then. For me (40 resting,
190 max), that gives a range of 133 to 152.5 (157 for long runs). Most my
regular runs are probably in the 140s, that just tends to feel right most of
the time.

That run more, run slower from before? That's all in that zone.

How much do I still use all that? Eeehh it depends. Most my regular running is
by feel. If I'm dead at the end, then I know I probably overdid it. I do still
keep a HR data field on my watch and I do still look at it often enough though.
Sometimes you'll get in a speedier zone, suddenly notice you're pushing the
upper 150s and, in my case, decide to reign it in some. Sometimes I see it's
been really rather low and test whether my legs are OK with speeding up a
little. For workouts, I will pay a bit more attention to it again... up to a
point. Once a workout gets too fast, the HR does not catch up quickly enough
and I do those by pace on a track instead. See the harder running section for
more.

## Sometimes You Run Longer

You want some different stimuli on your running. A staple is the long run. Once
a week go quite a bit longer than the rest of your runs. Mine tends to be
2-2h30. Depending on how much you run, that can be way too much already ofc,
keep it sane. Maybe you pick another day or two where you run a bit longer (but
not as long as the long run). Going to depend more on the time you can spend on
it and what your body can handle.

## Sometimes You Run Harder

(and/or longer)

To get faster, you do want to run faster at times. Different coaches have
different ideas of what intensities are most important. They all give them
different names, it's a mess. Again, not an exact science. You can get more
exact with lactate measurements (costly), power is gaining interest in the
community (need certain footpods), and recent race results + a track is pretty
precise too. However _what_ you choose to do will still be somewhat inexact.

### What

I have some options from ["Daniels Running Formula"
listed](/books/jack-daniels-running-formula.html). For all of the workouts, you
will want to do warmup and cooldown and often there is a rest (jog, ...)
between reps and/or sets. Particularly in my training, in increasing order of
speed, I tend to do:

- Threshold runs (aka tempo runs aka lactate threshold). For example 2×10 mins
  or whatever my body has been used to recently. That page has more details.
  These I do a bit more by feel + general idea of pace + general idea of HR. As
  such I can do them on the street or wherever. People often describe it as
  "comfortably hard".
- Interval / VO2max workouts. These I do on a track or something similar that
  is flat and reasonably well measured. I do them exclusively by pace, based on
  recent race results and/or previous workouts. Hence the track, HR is too slow
  to catch up, GPS too wonky.
- Repetition. Always on a track, also always by pace. During earlier COVID
  lockdowns I've made do with a bike path and a guesstimate of the distance
  (putting a marker there), but a track is much preferred.

Sometimes I also do CV or Critical Velocity training, I have [some notes on
that too](/notes/critical-velocity.html).

If you have the motivation, you can read books / articles about different
coaches and their training approach. There's honestly a lot of options out
there, but it will always come down to picking a certain speed that is faster
than regular running and doing it repeatedly in one of your days of running.

### When

Most your running will be easy still. Pick a day (or two if you've got some
more experience with workouts) to do one of the workouts. Can combine it with a
day you run a bit longer too (but usually not with the long run).

## Strides

These aren't really a workout, but they are a little extra stimulus. Helps you
with the feel of running fast basically. Basically do about 20s sprint like 6
times. Jog till fully rested inbetween. It's not an all out sprint, but it'll
be pretty close? Hard to describe. You should not feel any tiredness from them
after. Can tack them on to most any run. I do them when I'm in the mood and
think about it. I don't do them often enough.

## Periodisation

You need some recovery from harder work. This works on many levels.

- Splitting up hard running in reps
- Splitting up hard days by putting an easier or rest day inbetween
- Every few weeks, run a bit less. I tend to do every fourth week, only running
  80% of what I would have otherwise run.
- Maybe there's a week here or there in a year that you just want to completely
  not run for a week (I don't do this, but supposedly others do sometimes)

Generally to peak for races, people build up their running: adding more
distance over time, adding more intense workouts over time. I don't really do
the distance build up any more, I cannot add much more timewise and I don't
want to drop in distance just to add more. I dunno if this hurts my peaking.
Workouts I do end up rebuilding often enough just because they are the first to
go in case of niggles or tiredness.

Before races, you also often drop your distance some to recover from the
training ("taper"). The longer the race, the longer the taper, generally.

## Throw It Together

So how do I combine all that? In full training, a regular week can look like
this. Or a 7 day block anyway. There's also some shifting in either direction
depending on real life.

- Mo: 10 km jog
- Tu: 17 km with a workout
- We: 13 km regular run
- Th: 18 km with a workout
- Fr: 13 km regular run
- Sa: 10 km jog
- Su: 27 km long

In terms of niggles or tiredness, workouts are the first to go, then distance
reduction, then downgrade to elliptical, then complete day off.

If you don't have a precise race goal, you can just juggle a bit with the
workouts you do. There are some that are considered better for certain
distances so you want to delve further into that if you do know what you want.
As you get better, you can add more reps and such.

Adjust it all to what fits for you of course. Maybe you want a day off every
week. Maybe you don't run enough to fill 7 days. Maybe... whatever else.
